The Ages

She's two. He's ten.Same volcano. Different week.

She's squeezing red food coloring into warm water. He's comparing eyewitness accounts of Pompeii. They're sitting at the same table, studying the same theme, and neither one is waiting for the other.

Ages 2 to 4
The Explorers
Warm water in a bin. Baking soda piled like snow. She squeezes red food coloring and watches the whole thing fizz. She doesn't know the word eruption yet. She just made one.
Learning is physical. Activities are built for small hands, short windows, and the kind of curiosity that puts things in its mouth. No reading required. No screens. Just material.
Ages 4 to 7
The Readers
He's sounding out the word on the card. M-A-G-M-A. He matches it to the picture. He holds it up: "I got magma." He's been doing this for twenty minutes and hasn't noticed.
This is where letters become words and words become power. Vocabulary cards, tracing, counting with real objects. They want to do everything the older kids are doing. The plan lets them.
Ages 8 to 10
The Connectors
She mapped the Ring of Fire before lunch. After snack, she measured Mount Fuji in doorways — 233 of them. Now she's writing three sentences using her vocabulary words and arguing that "tectonic" should count as two.
They're starting to see how things relate. Math connects to geography. Science explains what history recorded. The plan doesn't just teach subjects — it gives them the thread between subjects to pull.
Ages 10 to 12
The Thinkers
Three accounts of Pompeii. One says the eruption lasted hours. Another says days. He's circled the contradictions and written in the margin: "They can't both be right." He hasn't been asked to do this.
They don't just learn facts — they interrogate them. Complex sources, original analysis, structured argument. This is where learning starts to look like thinking. The plan meets them there.

Four kids. One table. Nobody's waiting
